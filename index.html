The problem

CSMs are flying blind without a prioritization system

In most SaaS companies, a Customer Success Manager carries a book of 50 to 150 accounts. Without a structured health scoring system, their week starts with a gut check — which accounts feel risky? Which calls feel overdue? The problem is that gut instinct is not a retention strategy.

Accounts churn quietly. The customer who stops opening your platform does not send a warning email. The account on a month-to-month contract that has never contacted support is not obviously at risk until they send a cancellation request. By then, the save play is reactive, rushed, and often too late.

"The best save play is the one you run before the customer decides to leave."

This tool is built to answer one question every Monday morning: which accounts need my attention this week, and what specifically should I do about each one?

It is not a reporting tool. It is not a dashboard for executives. It is a prioritization and action system for a working CSM — built on real customer data, with every signal weight justified by what actually drives churn.

Health score methodology

Every weight has a reason

The health score is a 0–100 composite calculated from six signals. Each signal was chosen because it maps to a known intervention point in the CS workflow — not because the data happened to be available. This is the distinction between a data project and a CS tool.

Signal Weight Why this signal matters
Contract type 25 pts The single strongest commitment signal available. A two-year contract represents a deliberate organizational decision to invest. A month-to-month account can cancel with 30 days notice — they require active engagement regardless of other signals.
Tenure 25 pts Longevity is the best proxy for product embedding. Accounts that have survived at least one renewal cycle have already made the decision to stay once. Accounts under 12 months are in the highest-risk window — they have not yet fully adopted the product and have not yet renewed.
MRR level 20 pts Higher-value accounts tend to have more organizational stakeholders involved in the purchase decision, creating natural retention forces. Low-MRR accounts on short contracts represent the highest churn probability combination in the dataset.
Tech support usage 15 pts A dark account — one that has never engaged support — is almost always a sign of non-adoption, not satisfaction. Engaged accounts surface issues. Disengaged accounts quietly cancel. This is the earliest leading indicator of churn in the dataset and the most actionable signal for a CSM.
Billing method 10 pts Automatic payment methods eliminate involuntary churn from failed transactions. Manual payment accounts have a measurable uplift in churn rate unrelated to product satisfaction — this is an easy win for a CSM to fix during any scheduled touchpoint.
Services count 5 pts Each additional service module increases the cost and effort of switching. An account using five of six available services has deeply integrated the platform. An account using one service has not — and can leave without disruption to their operations.

The churn risk score is sourced directly from the IBM dataset's pre-built model (0–100, higher = more likely to churn). This is treated as a second independent signal — a high health score paired with a high churn risk score flags an account that looks good on paper but has behavioral indicators of flight risk. These are the most important accounts to call.

Natural extension

Where this goes next

This tool is built on a static dataset. In a real CS environment, the next step would be connecting it to live CRM data — Salesforce, HubSpot, or Gainsight — so health scores update automatically as accounts evolve. Here is how I would extend this in a production context.

AI-generated account narratives

The health score tells a CSM which accounts need attention. But translating a score into a customer conversation still requires interpretation. The natural extension is using an LLM to generate a 2–3 sentence plain-language account narrative for each record — something like: "This account has been active for 12 months but has never engaged support and is on manual billing. Based on these signals, the recommended play is a value realization call before the next billing cycle." That is the workflow Gainsight Copilot and Salesforce Einstein are now offering as premium features. Building it from scratch demonstrates that I understand the underlying logic, not just the software.

Live CRM integration

In production, health scores would recalculate nightly from Salesforce data — pulling contract dates, support tickets, product usage logs, and payment history automatically. The CSM would open the dashboard each morning to a prioritized list of accounts whose scores had changed since the previous day. The architecture is identical to what is built here — the data source is just a live API instead of a CSV.

Churn reason analysis

The dataset includes a churn reason field for every account that has already churned — competitor offer, price, dissatisfaction, moved, and so on. A natural extension would be mapping these reasons back to the health score signals to identify which combinations of signals most reliably predict each churn reason. That would make the recommended play column more specific: not just "re-engage" but "re-engage — competitor risk, focus on differentiation."
